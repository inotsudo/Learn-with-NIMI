# Level 1 · Unit 1 — Hello, World!
## Authored Lesson Content (Gold Standard — BK.4B.1)

**Level:** 1 — Toddler (18 months – 3 years)
**Block:** 1 — Identity & Welcome
**Unit Theme:** Hello, World!
**Development Focus:** First greetings, self-awareness, and beginning social interaction
**Unit Learning Outcomes:**
1. Respond to their own name when called
2. Imitate a simple hello gesture (wave, smile)
3. Hear and recognise basic greetings in English, French, and Kinyarwanda

## Lesson 1 — Morning Song

### 🎵 Morning Song — "Hello, Hello!"

**Blueprint Objective:** Sing a call-and-response "Hello" song using name and wave gesture
**Mission Type:** `sing`
**Duration:** 5–7 minutes

#### Learning Objective
The child waves their hand and says or approximates "hello" in response to hearing their name sung in the chorus.

#### Materials
- No materials required
- Optional: a puppet or soft toy to "sing along with"

#### Activity Instructions
1. Sit with your child facing you, close enough to make eye contact.
2. Begin singing the verse slowly, waving your own hand on the action cues.
3. When you reach the chorus, insert your child's name clearly and look directly at them.
4. Pause after singing their name and wait for their wave or smile — celebrate any response.
5. Repeat the chorus 2–3 times, using their name each time.
6. On the final round, hold their hand and wave it together.

#### Parent Guidance
This song works best when you make big, exaggerated facial expressions and wave enthusiastically. Your child learns by watching you first, so model the gestures before expecting them to copy. If your child just smiles instead of waving, that is a perfect response for this age — celebrate it as a full success.

#### Success Criteria
| Level | Observable behaviour |
|---|---|
| Emerging | Makes eye contact and stills when their name is sung |
| Developing | Smiles or vocalises during the chorus |
| Mastered | Waves hand when their name is sung in the chorus |

#### Language Versions

##### English
- **Title:** Hello, Hello!
- **Subtitle:** A call-and-response greeting song with waves and smiles
- **Tip Text:** Sing this every morning this week! Use your child's real name in the chorus each time — hearing their own name in a song helps them recognize it and builds identity.

## Lesson 2 — Movement Mission

### 🏃 Movement Mission — "Come to Me!"

**Blueprint Objective:** Waddle, wave, and walk toward a caregiver on cue in a "Come to me!" movement game
**Mission Type:** `move`
**Duration:** 5–8 minutes

#### Learning Objective
The child walks or crawls toward their caregiver when called and waves their hand as a greeting on cue.

#### Materials
- Clear floor space of at least 2 metres
- Optional: a colourful scarf or ribbon to wave

#### Activity Instructions
1. Stand or crouch about 2 metres away from your child.
2. Open your arms wide and say the first prompt: "Wave your hand — say hello!"
3. Wait for your child to wave, then immediately give the second prompt: "Now come to me!"
4. Cheer and clap as they move toward you.
5. When they arrive, celebrate: "You gave me a hello! Amazing!"
6. Carry out a big smile and hug as the closing celebration.
7. Repeat 3–4 times, increasing distance slightly each round.

#### Parent Guidance
This game builds the connection between greeting language and a physical social gesture — fundamental for early social development. Let your child set the pace; if they want to run rather than "waddle," that is fine — the key outcome is the greeting gesture and the social turn-taking. Keep your energy high: your enthusiasm is what makes this irresistible for a toddler.

#### Success Criteria
| Level | Observable behaviour |
|---|---|
| Emerging | Looks toward the caregiver when called |
| Developing | Moves toward the caregiver in any way (crawl, walk, toddle) |
| Mastered | Moves toward caregiver AND attempts a wave or gesture on cue |

#### Language Versions

##### English
- **Title:** Come to Me!
- **Subtitle:** A walk-and-wave greeting game
- **Tip Text:** Say "wave" and wave yourself first — toddlers learn by copying! Play this game as a daily hello ritual at drop-off, pick-up, or wake-up time to build the greeting habit.

## Lesson 3 — Mission Artistique

### 🎨 Mission Artistique — "My Hello Hand"

**Blueprint Objective:** Make a hand-print greeting card using a single bold colour
**Mission Type:** `color`
**Duration:** 7–10 minutes

#### Materials
- Washable, non-toxic finger paint — one bold colour (red, yellow, or blue)
- Large sheet of white or light-coloured paper (A4 or A3)
- A shallow tray or plate to pour paint into
- Wet cloth for clean-up
- Optional: a marker to write the child's name after

#### Activity Instructions
1. Set up the paint tray on a stable surface at your child's height.
2. Say: "We are going to make a hello hand! This is how we say hello with our hands."
3. Demonstrate: press your own hand in the paint and stamp it on the paper.
4. Guide your child's hand gently into the paint — let them feel it.
5. Count together: "One, two, three — stamp!" as you press their hand to the paper.
6. Lift up to reveal the print: "Your hello hand!"
7. While the paint dries, point to each finger: "Hello, finger! Hello, finger!"
8. When dry, write "Hello from [child's name]!" beneath the print.

#### Parent Guidance
Finger paint can feel strange to some toddlers — if your child is reluctant, start by showing them on your own hand first and let them touch your paint-covered hand before trying theirs. One print is a complete success; do not pressure them to repeat it. The talking-while-doing (naming fingers, saying "stamp!") is where the language learning happens — keep the commentary flowing even if the child is quiet.

#### Success Criteria
| Level | Observable behaviour |
|---|---|
| Emerging | Tolerates paint on hand without pulling away |
| Developing | Participates in stamping with hand-over-hand support |
| Mastered | Stamps independently and points to their print with recognition |

#### Language Versions

##### English
- **Title:** My Hello Hand
- **Subtitle:** Stamp your hand and send a greeting!
- **Tip Text:** Use ONE bold colour for a clean, beautiful print. While stamping, count "one, two, three — stamp!" every time — the counting rhythm is calming for toddlers and builds early number sense at the same time.
- **Content JSON:**
```json
{
  "instructions": "Dip your hand in the paint. Press it onto the paper. Lift it up slowly — there's your hello hand! Point to each finger and say: hello, hello, hello, hello, hello! One hello for every finger!"
}
```

##### Français
- **Title:** Ma Main Bonjour
- **Subtitle:** Tamponne ta main et envoie un bonjour !
- **Tip Text:** Utilisez UNE seule couleur vive pour une belle empreinte nette. En tamponnant, comptez « un, deux, trois — hop ! » à chaque fois — le rythme du comptage est apaisant et développe la conscience des chiffres.
- **Content JSON:**
```json
{
  "instructions": "Plonge ta main dans la peinture. Appuie-la sur le papier. Soulève-la doucement — voilà ta main bonjour ! Pointe chaque doigt et dis : bonjour, bonjour, bonjour, bonjour, bonjour ! Un bonjour pour chaque doigt !"
}
```

##### Kinyarwanda
- **Title:** Ukuboko Kwanjye kwa Muraho
- **Subtitle:** Shira akaboko mu mafuta uhereze abantu muraho!
- **Tip Text:** Koresha UBURURU bumwe gusa kugirango uruzinduko ruhere neza. Mugihe ushira mu mafuta, baza "imwe, ebyiri, eshatu — shira!" buri gihe — urugero rwo kubara rurakura inzirakarengane kandi rugaca ibibazo.

## Lesson 4 — Mission Historique

### 🏛️ Mission Historique — "Baby Amara's Morning"

**Blueprint Objective:** Listen to a brief story about a baby's first morning and first greeting with family
**Mission Type:** `read`
**Duration:** 5–7 minutes

#### Materials
- No materials required
- Optional: show a simple illustration of a Rwandan home interior while reading

#### Activity Instructions
1. Sit your child comfortably in your lap or beside you.
2. Say: "I am going to tell you a story about Baby Amara. She lives in Rwanda, just like us."
3. Read the story slowly, pausing after each sentence.
4. Act out the wave when Amara waves — encourage your child to copy.
5. Use a warm, smiling voice for Mama's lines.
6. After the story, ask: "Can you wave like Amara? Hello!"

#### Parent Guidance
Repeat this story daily for the entire week — toddlers love repetition and will begin to anticipate key moments (especially the wave). By the third or fourth reading, your child may wave before you even reach that sentence. That anticipation is a sign of early comprehension. If your child points at the page or vocalises during reading, pause and acknowledge: "Yes! Amara is waving! Just like you!"

#### Success Criteria
| Level | Observable behaviour |
|---|---|
| Emerging | Stays engaged for the full story without distracting |
| Developing | Waves when Amara waves in the story |
| Mastered | Waves at the correct moment AND vocalises (e.g. says "hi" or "mama") |

#### Language Versions

##### English
- **Title:** Baby Amara's Morning
- **Subtitle:** A story about a baby's first hello
- **Tip Text:** Read this story slowly and act out the wave! After a few days your child will wave along before you even get to that part — that's early comprehension. Use the same warm, happy voice every time to make this a cozy read-aloud ritual.
- **Content JSON:**
```json
{
  "text": "This is Amara. She is a baby in Rwanda. She wakes up in the morning. The sun is shining. She sees Mama. Amara waves her hand. Hello, Mama! Mama smiles big. Hello, Amara! I love you! Amara is happy. She waves again. Hello, world!"
```

## Lesson 5 — Mission Zoom

### 🔍 Mission Zoom — "Hello, Rwanda!"

**Blueprint Objective:** Watch smiling faces from Rwanda saying "Muraho", "Bonjour", "Hello" in sequence
**Mission Type:** `watch`
**Duration:** 4–6 minutes

#### Materials
- Device with screen (tablet or phone)
- Optional: a small hand mirror to use alongside the video

#### Activity Instructions
1. Sit beside your child so you can both see the screen.
2. Before starting, say: "We are going to watch friends from Rwanda say hello! Can you say hello with them?"
3. Play the video/show the greeting sequence.
4. Each time a person on screen waves, pause and say: "Wave! Wave like them!" and wave yourself.
5. After the video, turn to your child and say all three greetings: "Hello! Bonjour! Muraho!"
6. Celebrate every time your child repeats a word or wave.

#### Parent Guidance
For this age, the video is a trigger for the greeting — the goal is not passive viewing but active call-and-response with you. Pause frequently and make eye contact with your child as you say the greeting words. It does not matter if they only pick up one of the three words this week — early multilingual exposure is cumulative, not immediate. Keep the mood celebratory.

#### Success Criteria
| Level | Observable behaviour |
|---|---|
| Emerging | Looks at the screen and watches the faces attentively |
| Developing | Waves along with one of the greeting sequences |
| Mastered | Vocalises or attempts one of the three greeting words (any language) |

#### Language Versions

##### English
- **Title:** Hello, Rwanda!
- **Subtitle:** Watch friendly faces say hello in three languages
- **Tip Text:** Pause the video and wave along every time! Say all three greetings out loud: "Hello! Bonjour! Muraho!" — your child learning all three is the goal, even if it takes many sessions.
- **Content JSON:**

## Lesson 6 — Mission Discovery

### 🌱 Mission Discovery — "Who Is That?"

**Blueprint Objective:** Touch a mirror and recognise their own reflection; point to their face
**Mission Type:** `watch`
**Duration:** 5–7 minutes

#### Materials
- A hand mirror or wall mirror (toddler-safe)
- Optional: a soft cloth to wipe the mirror before and after

#### Activity Instructions
1. Hold the mirror so your child can see themselves clearly.
2. Say: "Who is that? Let's look!" — pause and let them look.
3. Point to their reflection: "Is that you? YES! That's YOU!"
4. Say: "Wave hello to yourself! Say: Hello, me!"
5. Guide their hand to wave at the mirror.
6. Point to features one by one: "Eyes! Nose! Mouth! Big smile!"
7. Turn the mirror to yourself: "And that's me! Hello!"
8. Repeat: hand the mirror back to your child and ask "Who is that?" again.

#### Parent Guidance
Self-recognition in a mirror typically develops between 18 and 24 months. If your child tries to look behind the mirror or seems confused, that is developmentally normal — simply continue naming their features warmly without correction. The repetition across multiple sessions will build toward recognition. Never test or quiz; keep the tone warm and playful throughout.

#### Success Criteria
| Level | Observable behaviour |
|---|---|
| Emerging | Looks at the mirror with sustained attention (10+ seconds) |
| Developing | Touches the mirror or their own face during the activity |
| Mastered | Smiles, waves, or vocalises at their own reflection — shows recognition |

#### Language Versions

##### English
- **Title:** Who Is That?
- **Subtitle:** Look in the mirror and say hello to YOU!
- **Tip Text:** Self-recognition in a mirror develops around 18–24 months. If your child tries to look behind the mirror, that is totally normal! Keep naming their features warmly: "eyes, nose, mouth, YOU!" — it all builds up over weeks.
- **Content JSON:**
```json
{
  "instructions": "Look in the mirror! Who is that? It's YOU! Wave hello to yourself. Say: Hello, me! Point to your eyes. Point to your nose. Point to your big smile! That is your hello face!"
}
```

- **Title:** C'est qui, ça ?
- **Subtitle:** Regarde dans le miroir et dis bonjour à TOI !
- **Tip Text:** La reconnaissance de soi dans un miroir se développe vers 18–24 mois. Si votre enfant cherche derrière le miroir, c'est tout à fait normal ! Continuez à nommer ses traits chaleureusement : « yeux, nez, bouche, TOI ! » — tout cela se construit au fil des semaines.

## Lesson 7 — FlipFlop Book

### 📖 FlipFlop Book — "Hello, Friend!"

**Blueprint Objective:** Flip through a "Hello, Friend!" picture book showing children greeting each other
**Mission Type:** `story`
**Duration:** 5–8 minutes

#### Materials
- No materials required (digital book rendered in app)
- Optional: point to illustrations on screen as each page is read

#### Activity Instructions
1. Settle your child comfortably and say: "Let's read a story about a little girl called Zara. She lives in Rwanda and loves to say hello!"
2. Read each page slowly and expressively.
3. On page 2, wave while you read "Zara waves her hand."
4. On page 4, prompt your child: "Can you wave? Wave with Zara!"
5. After the last page, say: "The end! Zara says hello to the whole world — just like you!"
6. Encourage your child to say or wave "hello" one final time.

#### Parent Guidance
For toddlers, "reading" a book means listening to your voice and watching your expressions — comprehension comes from rhythm, tone, and repetition, not word-for-word understanding. Read this book multiple times this week. By the second or third reading, point to the characters and ask "Where's Zara?" — pointing comprehension develops faster than verbal comprehension at this age.

| Emerging | Stays engaged through the full 5 pages without turning away |

## Lesson 8 — Coloring Book

### 🖍️ Coloring Book — "My Smiley Face"

**Blueprint Objective:** Colour a large, simple smiling face template
**Mission Type:** `color`
**Duration:** 5–8 minutes

#### Materials
- Printed or displayed smiling face template (large, simple outlines only)
- Washable crayons or chunky markers — 2–3 colours maximum
- Flat surface at child's height

#### Activity Instructions
1. Place the template in front of your child and say: "Look — a smiley face! This face is saying hello!"
2. Hold up a crayon and say: "Let's give it some colour. What colour shall we choose?"
3. Help your child hold the crayon (fist grip is fine at this age).
4. As they colour, narrate: "You're colouring the eyes! Hello, eyes! You're colouring the mouth! Big smiiiile!"
5. When finished, hold up the picture: "Your smiley face is saying HELLO! Hello from your picture!"
6. Ask: "Can you give the picture a wave? Hello, smiley face!"

#### Parent Guidance
At this age, colouring "inside the lines" is not the goal and should not be mentioned — the scribbling process itself is the developmental work. The goal is the parallel language: you narrating "eyes," "mouth," "hello" as they colour builds vocabulary through context. Keep the praise focused on the process, not the product: "I love how you're colouring!" rather than "That looks so good!"

#### Success Criteria
| Level | Observable behaviour |
|---|---|
| Emerging | Picks up a crayon and makes any mark on the template |
| Developing | Colours a recognisable section (eye area, mouth area) with intent |
| Mastered | Completes the face and points to features when named by the caregiver |

#### Language Versions
